LU XUN

A leading figure of modern Chinese literature

Here is a time line of Lu Xun

  • 1881 - Born in Shaoxing, Zhejiang, China
  • 1899 - Studies at a tuition-free military school in Nanjing, the "Jiangnan Naval Academy"
  • 1902 - Leaves home to Japan on a Qing government's scholarship to pursue an education in Western medicine
  • 1906 - Terminates his pursuit of the degree and leaves college. He begins to read Nietzsche, and writes a number of essays in the period that are influenced by his philosophy. In June 1906, Lu marries a girl named Zhu An under his mother's arrangement.
  • 1909 - Publishes a translation of a eastern european fiction, Tales from Abroad. Between 1909–1911 Lu holds a number of brief teaching positions at local colleges and secondary schools
  • 1912 - Shortly after the Xinhai Revolution that ends the Qing dynasty and nominally founds the Republic of China, Lu gains a position at the National Ministry of Education. He is hired in Nanjing, but then moves with the ministry to Beijing, where he lives from 1912–1926
  • 1917 - In 1917, an old friend of Lu's, Qian Xuantong, invites Lu to write for a radical populist literary magazine called New Youth. Lu writes for the magazine, and produces his most famous stories for New Youth between 1917–1921
  • 1918 - Writes his first short story published in his name, Diary of a Madman, for the magazine New Youth
  • 1920 - Begins to lecture part-time at several colleges, including Peking University, Beijing Normal University, and Beijing Women’s College, where he teaches traditional fiction and literary theory. His lecture notes are later collected and published as a A Brief History of Chinese Fiction
  • 1921 - Writes an episodic novella, The True Story of Ah Q
  • 1925 - Founds a journal, Wilderness, and establishes the "Weiming Society" in order to support young writers and encourage the translation of foreign literature into Chinese
  • 1926 - In March 1926 there is a mass student protest against the warlord Feng Yuxiang's collaboration with the Japanese. The protest degenerates into a massacre, in which two of Lu's students from Beijing Women's College are killed. Lu's public support for the protesters forces him to flee from the local authorities. Later in 1926, when the warlord troops of Zhang Zuolin and Wu Peifu takes over Beijing, Lu leaves northern China and flees to Xiamen
  • 1927 - Is considered for the Nobel Prize in Literature, for the short story The True Story of Ah Q. In January 1927, Lu moves to Guangzhou, where he is hired as the head of the Zhongshan University Chinese literature department. After the Shanghai massacre in April 1927, he attempts to secure the release of several students through the university, but failed. His failure to save his students leads him to resign from his position at the university, and he leaves for the foreign settlement of Shanghai in September 1927
  • 1929 - Visites his dying mother, and reported the news of Guangping's pregnancy, with whom Lu has his first meaningful romantic relationship. Xu Guangping gave birth to a son, Haiying, on September 27 1929
  • 1930 - Becomes one of the co-founders of the League of Left-Wing Writers
  • 1934 - Publishes his last collection of short stories, Old Tales Retold
  • 1936 - Dies of tuberculosis at the age of 55 in Shanghai
